Korea’s Political Polarization: Lee Jae Myung And Kim Moon Soo, The Two Candidates For The 21st Presidential Election Can you believe Korea’s democracy level has been downgraded in the “flawed democracy” category? According to an Economist Intelligence Unit (EIU) report, Korea’s democracy has been rated “flawed” after martial law from the “full democracy” classification, which is a status Korea had maintained since 2020. EIU attributed the downgrade to political instability linked to the 20th president Yoon’s botched martial law and the resulting political deadlock (Korea's democracy rated 'flawed' after martial law: Report, 2025). In the aftermath of martial law, Korea held a more urgent presidential election. The two subjects that I’m interested in were Lee Jae-myung and Kim Moon-soo. The competition between Lee Jae-myung and Kim Moon-soo in the 21st presidential election clearly revealed the polarization. In this essay, I’m going to show how polarized Korean society is, what makes it polarized, and how it should be resolved by looking deep into the similarities and differences between the two candidates for the 21st presidential election, Lee Jae-myung and Kim Moon-soo. Lee Jae-myung and Kim Moon-soo share some notable similarities. First, both ran in the 21st presidential election to retrieve the political chaos right after martial law. Lee was the presidential candidate of the Democratic Party, and Kim represented the People’s Power Party. Also, their political parties are the main parties that control Korean politics. Second, both have successful Gyeonggi Province governor careers. Both laid the foundation for Gyeonggi Province’s transportation and industrial development as provincial governors. Finally, both agreed on how severely polarized Korean society is in the 21st presidential debate. In the debate, they pointed to power-exclusive party lines to annihilate the other side as the main cause of polarization (Yonhap Infomax, 2025). Looking at these similarities, it seems possible to make consensus and harmony. However, how severely polarized is Korean society? And how should we fix it? To answer these questions, let’s contrast Lee Jae-myung and Kim Moon-soo. The first difference is their contrasting stances regarding the impeachment of the 20th president Yoon Suk-yeol. Lee urged the Constitutional Court to impeach the 20th president Yoon Suk-yeol (Lee Jae-myung urges Constitutional Court to impeach President Yoon Suk-yeol, 2025). In contrast, anti-impeachment candidate Kim Moon-soo took issue with the Constitutional Court’s unanimous ruling in Yoon’s impeachment case (Kim Moon-soo compares Yoon impeachment to 'communist states like North Korea', 2025). Even though, based on the Constitutional Court’s ruling, martial law under Yoon Suk-yeol was an illegal coup against the Constitution, and his impeachment was a legitimate result, the two contrasting stances clearly show how severely polarized South Korea is. The second difference is their political supporters. Lee’s supporters consist of progressive reformers and the working class, whereas Kim’s are made up of the traditional conservative base and Christian forces. Their supporters are extremely hostile to each other. For example, supporters of each candidate call each other “1jjik” and “2jjik,” expressing their hate without hesitation. They criticize each other for supporting the opposing party, saying, “What happened to your mind?” or using insulting words such as “trash” and “parasite.” Their political supporters clearly demonstrate how serious the polarization of Korea is. The last difference is their solutions to political polarization. Lee proposed the expansion of proportional representation and the introduction of runoff voting, which would create the possibility of a multiparty system and end the two-party monopoly. On the other hand, Kim argued that limited reform within the current framework with proper leadership is more practical than drastic overhaul. Even though Lee’s reform might have social costs, it mainly targets the two-party system’s monopoly, whereas Kim’s proposal does not provide specific ways to address it. Therefore, I support Lee Jae-myung’s alternative proposals. In conclusion, even though Lee and Kim have some notable similarities and it seems possible to achieve consensus and reconciliation, their differences, which cannot be narrowed, clearly show how severely polarized Korean society is. When it comes to the main cause of polarization, both agree the two-party system contributes to division, but they propose quite different alternatives. Since Lee’s proposal specifically targets the main cause, I believe it is a more appropriate way to fix it than Kim’s.
